# Medical Personal Statement Writer

A comprehensive Claude skill for guiding medical school applicants through writing compelling personal statements using Dr. Ryan Gray's proven methodology from "The Premed Playbook Guide to the Medical School Personal Statement."

## What This Skill Does

This skill helps medical school applicants:
- Discover their core story and motivations for medicine
- Transform "telling" statements into vivid, memorable "showing"
- Avoid common pitfalls that weaken personal statements
- Structure a compelling narrative that answers "why physician?"
- Edit and refine drafts with specific, actionable feedback
- Meet character limits for AMCAS (5,300), AACOMAS (4,500), and TMDSAS (5,000)

## Installation

1. Clone or download this repository
2. Place the `medical-ps-writer` folder in your Claude skills directory:
   - **Claude.ai:** Upload via the skills interface
   - **Claude Code:** Place in `~/.claude/skills/` (Mac/Linux) or `%USERPROFILE%\.claude\skills\` (Windows)

## Core Methodology

Based on Dr. Ryan Gray's proven approach:

### Key Principles
1. **Show, Don't Tell** - Use vivid sensory details instead of summarizing
2. **Why Physician?** - Clearly articulate why you need to be a doctor specifically
3. **Not a Resume** - Focus on reflection and meaning, not listing experiences
4. **Specific Moments** - Use particular instances instead of generalizations
5. **Self-Reflection** - Go beyond "I want to help people"

### The Process
1. Discover your core story through guided questions
2. Identify your most impactful experiences
3. Create a compelling opening hook
4. Show your experiences through sensory storytelling
5. Demonstrate deep self-reflection
6. Avoid common pitfalls
7. Structure for maximum impact
8. Edit systematically

## Example Transformation

### Before (Telling)
"I volunteered at a clinic and it was a great experience. I learned that I enjoy helping patients and want to be a doctor."

### After (Showing)
"Mrs. Rodriguez's weathered hands shook as she tried to sign the intake form. 'Lo siento, my English...not so good,' she whispered, eyes downcast. I pulled my chair closer, and together we worked through each question. When we finished, she squeezed my hand and smiled—the first time I'd seen her face relax. In that moment, sitting in the cramped clinic office with its flickering fluorescent lights, I understood that medicine wasn't just about curing disease. It was about being present for someone when they felt most vulnerable."

## Best Practices

1. **Start Early** - Don't wait until the last minute
2. **Be Honest** - The skill can only work with authentic experiences
3. **Iterate** - Expect multiple drafts and revisions
4. **Get Feedback** - Use the skill's guidance alongside human reviewers
5. **Take Breaks** - Step away and return with fresh eyes
