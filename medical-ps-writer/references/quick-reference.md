# Quick Reference Guide

## The Two Critical Questions

1. **"What was the first time you questioned if being a physician was right for you?"**
   - This identifies your spark/origin moment

2. **"What experiences have continued to strengthen your desire to become a physician?"**
   - This identifies your confirmation experiences

## The Show, Don't Tell Formula

### TELLING Template
"I [verb] and [it was adjective]. I learned that [lesson]."

### SHOWING Template
"[Specific person] [specific action/dialogue]. [Sensory detail]. [Your action/response]. [What happened]. [Implied lesson through the story]."

## Five Senses Checklist

When describing an experience, try to include:
- [ ] **Sight** - What did you see? (expressions, colors, movements)
- [ ] **Sound** - What did you hear? (voices, equipment, environment)
- [ ] **Touch** - What did you feel? (textures, temperatures, sensations)
- [ ] **Smell** - What smells were present? (antiseptic, etc.)
- [ ] **Taste** - Rarely used but powerful when relevant

## Common TELLING Red Flags

Watch for these phrases in your draft:
- "I learned that..."
- "This showed me..."
- "I realized..."
- "I am passionate about..."
- "This made me want..."
- "I have always been..."
- Using adjectives instead of examples

## The "Why Physician" Test

Read your statement and replace every instance of "physician" or "doctor" with:
- Nurse
- Physician Assistant
- Social Worker
- Healthcare professional

**If it still makes sense, you haven't explained why PHYSICIAN specifically.**

## Must Include Elements

✅ Specific moment that sparked interest
✅ Actions you took to explore medicine
✅ Vivid scene with sensory details
✅ Clear explanation of why PHYSICIAN (not other healthcare)
✅ Self-reflection beyond "I want to help people"
✅ Opening hook that grabs attention
✅ Strong closing that ties back to beginning

## Must Avoid Elements

❌ Resume listing of experiences
❌ "I want to help people" without specificity
❌ Clichéd opening phrases
❌ Claiming you already have doctor skills
❌ Science lectures to show knowledge
❌ Topics that make you cry
❌ Lies or exaggerations
❌ Overly complex vocabulary
❌ Chronological timeline structure

## Character Limits

- **AMCAS:** 5,300 characters
- **AACOMAS:** 4,500 characters
- **TMDSAS:** 5,000 characters

## Paragraph Structure (Recommended)

1. **Hook** (800-1000 chars): Vivid opening moment
2. **Origin** (600-800 chars): What sparked your interest
3. **Exploration** (1200-1500 chars): What you did to confirm + SHOWING
4. **Deepening** (1000-1200 chars): What you learned about yourself
5. **Why Physician** (800-1000 chars): Specific to physician role
6. **Closing** (600-800 chars): Tie back to opening

## Quick Quality Self-Check

Rate each on a scale of 1-5:

1. Does my opening grab attention immediately?
2. Am I SHOWING through specific scenes, not TELLING?
3. Is it crystal clear why I need to be a PHYSICIAN?
4. Have I gone beyond "I want to help people"?
5. Would this make someone want to interview me?
6. Is every sentence necessary?
7. Are there specific names, moments, and details?
8. Have I avoided all common mistakes?
9. Does it sound authentically like me?
10. Is it memorable?

**If you scored less than 4 on any item, revise that element.**

## Editing Rounds

### Round 1: Content
- Does every sentence support "why physician"?
- SHOWING vs. TELLING check
- Depth of reflection

### Round 2: Structure
- Logical flow
- Strong transitions
- Paragraph organization

### Round 3: Clarity
- Remove jargon
- Simplify complex sentences
- Check character count

### Round 4: Polish
- Read aloud
- Grammar/spelling
- Final review

## Emergency Fixes for Common Issues

### "My statement is boring"
→ Add specific sensory details, dialogue, and particular moments

### "It's too long"
→ Remove any experience that doesn't directly support "why physician"
→ Convert multi-sentence reflections to implied lessons through showing

### "It sounds generic"
→ Replace every adjective with a specific example
→ Add real names of people and places
→ Include dialogue

### "I'm telling too much"
→ For each paragraph, identify the moment and SHOW that scene
→ Remove "I learned" and "I realized" - show the lesson through the story

### "Why physician isn't clear"
→ Add a paragraph specifically addressing what PHYSICIANS do that other healthcare providers don't
→ Include: diagnostic responsibility, comprehensive training, team leadership, longitudinal care

## The Ultimate Test

**Read your statement aloud.**

Can you SEE it like a movie? Can you picture yourself there?
- YES → You're showing
- NO → You're telling (revise)

Does it clearly answer "Why do you want to be a physician?"
- YES → Good to go
- NO → Add specific paragraph addressing this

Would you want to interview this person?
- YES → Ready to submit
- NO → Keep revising

## Templates for Strong Statements

### Template A: The Moment
[Vivid specific moment] → [Brief context] → [How you explored] → [What you learned about yourself] → [Why physician] → [Tie back to moment]

### Template B: The Journey
[Initial spark] → [Specific experience 1 with SHOWING] → [Reflection] → [Specific experience 2 with SHOWING] → [Deeper reflection] → [Why physician] → [Future vision]

### Template C: The Transformation
[Where you started] → [Pivotal moment with SHOWING] → [What changed] → [How you confirmed the change] → [What this revealed] → [Why physician] → [Who you are now]

Final checklist:

- [ ] No spelling/grammar errors?
- [ ] Within character limit?
- [ ] Opening hooks the reader?
- [ ] Primarily SHOWING, not TELLING?
- [ ] Clear why PHYSICIAN specifically?
- [ ] Deep self-reflection included?

- [ ] Closed strongly and memorably?
- [ ] Would you interview this person?

## Getting Unstuck

**"I don't know where to start"**
→ Answer the two critical questions
→ Free-write about your most memorable medical experiences
→ Don't worry about structure yet - just get ideas down

**"I have too many experiences to choose from"**
→ Pick the 2-3 that most shaped your WHY
→ Quality over quantity - go deep, not broad

**"I can't think of specific details"**
→ Close your eyes and mentally return to the moment
→ What did the room look like? What sounds were there?
→ What was said? What did you physically feel?

**"This sounds like everyone else's"**
→ Add YOUR specific sensory details
→ Include YOUR particular realizations
→ Use real names and specific dialogue

**"I'm stuck on the opening"**
→ Write the rest first, come back to opening
→ Find your strongest SHOWING moment - start there
→ Avoid: "Since childhood..." "I have always..." "Medicine is..."

---

**Remember:** The goal is to make the reader understand WHY you want to be a physician and WHO you are. Everything else is secondary.
